# Riley's_Office — Office World Direction

> Project note: `PROJECT.md` and `BRAND.md` are now present in the project root and should be treated as active implementation context. This office-world direction builds on the existing premium visual, world-design, and isometric scene docs.

## Objective
Redesign the HQ and project environments so they read immediately as a **real executive office interior** rather than abstract geometry or generic glowing blocks.

The user should recognize, at a glance:
- architecture: walls, windows, floors, ceilings, doorways
- furniture: desks, chairs, monitors, tables, shelving, partitions
- workplace hierarchy: lobby, corridor, meeting room, bullpen, private office, workstation
- premium tone: modern, expensive, nocturnal, controlled

## Experience Thesis
Riley's_Office should feel like a luxury headquarters after hours: a real office floor with glass partitions, walnut or smoked-stone surfaces, monitor-lit workstations, and city-facing windows.

It must **not** feel like:
- floating sci-fi tiles with labels
- toy office illustration
- empty dark void with glowing cards
- abstract game board with no building logic

---

# 1. Spatial Identity Shift

## Current problem to solve
The current HQ language reads more like luminous district geometry than a believable office environment.

## New read
The world should read as a **single premium office floorplate** containing recognizable zones:
1. reception / arrival edge
2. main corridor spine
3. central operations floor
4. glass meeting room(s)
5. executive office / project suites
6. support zones: lounge credenza, print/storage wall, archive shelving

## Core rule
Every major interactive area must sit inside visible architecture.

That means scenes need recognizable:
- floor surfaces
- perimeter walls or partial walls
- window bands / mullions
- doorway openings or corridor thresholds
- furniture anchored to the floor

Do not present project modules as objects floating on a blank plane.

---

# 2. Architectural Language

## 2.1 Floorplate
The HQ should be built as one coherent office interior, not separate detached pads.

### Required floor cues
- continuous main floor surface
- visible change in flooring between circulation and workstation zones
- perimeter base shadow where walls meet floor
- inset area rugs only in premium spaces like executive office or meeting area

### Material recommendation
- circulation spine: dark smoked stone or polished charcoal concrete
- workstation zones: walnut or premium wood plank insert fields
- executive / meeting zones: darker carpet tile or acoustic rug fields for softness

## 2.2 Walls and partitions
Use office partitions to explain space.

### Required wall vocabulary
- full-height perimeter walls only at outer shell where needed
- glass meeting-room walls with metal frames
- half-height acoustic partitions around desk clusters
- back walls for delivery wall / archive wall / ops shelving

### Rules
- at least one visible rear or side wall in every room scene
- glass walls should reveal silhouettes beyond them
- avoid closed box rooms that kill openness
- avoid pure open-plan emptiness with no spatial boundaries

## 2.3 Windows
Windows are mandatory because they instantly make the scene feel like a real office.

### Window system
- large exterior window band on at least one side of HQ / key rooms
- vertical mullions or slim black frames
- subtle exterior city glow or night skyline wash
- reflected interior light on glass

### Purpose
Windows should provide:
- scale
- orientation
- premium atmosphere
- believable perimeter architecture

## 2.4 Ceiling implication
Do not render full literal ceilings everywhere, but imply them through:
- light coves
- overhead beam shadows
- pendant or recessed linear light pools
- conference-room light rectangles

This helps the office read as interior space, not an open void.

# 3. Office Furniture System

## 3.1 Desk types
Use multiple recognizable desk types so the office looks designed, not cloned.

### A. Executive run desk
Used for HQ core or primary project lead zone.

Characteristics:
- larger footprint than regular desks
- premium slab top
- integrated monitor rail or display wall
- flanking credenza / storage unit
- central placement

### B. Team workstation bench
Used for active agent clusters.

Characteristics:
- 2-seat or 4-seat shared benching system
- desk divider / acoustic screen
- dual monitor silhouettes
- keyboard/mouse zone cues
- under-desk chair placement

### C. Specialist corner desk
Used for important agents or highlighted workstations.

Characteristics:
- L-desk or side return
- 2–3 monitor arrangement
- evidence tray or sideboard
- stronger lamp / task-light cue

### D. Meeting table
Used for project review / QA / reviewer / delivery moments.

Characteristics:
- elongated table with 4–8 chairs
- central screen or wall display nearby
- glass room enclosure preferred

## 3.2 Seating
Chairs matter because they immediately humanize the office.

Include:
- high-back task chairs at workstations
- lower guest chairs at meeting tables
- occasional lounge chairs in waiting/lounge areas

Do not omit seating; rooms without chairs read like abstract installations.

## 3.3 Monitor language
Monitors are one of the strongest office signals.

### Rules
- most desks should have 1–3 monitor silhouettes
- active desks get brighter screen glow
- blocked desks may show a warmer warning strip on one display
- meeting rooms should include a wall display or presentation screen

## 3.4 Storage and support furniture
To avoid the scene feeling hollow, include background office support pieces:
- low credenzas
- shelving wall or archive shelving
- printer / utility cabinet zone
- planters or acoustic dividers used sparingly
- whiteboard or glass write-up wall in collaboration zones

# 4. Recognizable Room Types

## 4.1 HQ arrival / reception edge
Purpose: establish that this is a real headquarters.

Required cues:
- branded plaque or reception wall
- floor transition from entry to main office
- one waiting bench or lounge chair pair
- indirect lighting

## 4.2 Central operations floor
Purpose: the main active office field.

Required cues:
- clustered workstations
- circulation path around desks
- visible monitor glow across multiple stations
- one dominant command island / run table

## 4.3 Glass meeting room
Purpose: collaboration and review.

Required cues:
- glass enclosure with framed edges
- meeting table and chairs
- wall display or whiteboard
- slightly quieter light level than operations floor

## 4.4 Executive / project office
Purpose: make high-priority project spaces feel premium and specific.

Required cues:
- stronger material richness
- larger desk / project table
- side credenza or shelving
- window adjacency or premium wall treatment

## 4.5 Delivery / archive wall zone
Purpose: give artifacts a believable physical home.

Required cues:
- wall-mounted panel system or shelving band
- grouped evidence slabs mounted like pinned deliverables
- nearby storage / filing / archive references

# 5. Premium Scene Composition Rules

## 5.1 Focal hierarchy
Each office scene needs one dominant furniture anchor.

Use one of:
- executive run desk
- command island
- meeting table
- hero workstation cluster

Everything else should support that anchor.

## 5.2 Foreground, midground, background
To make the environment feel rich, compose every scene in three readable layers.

### Foreground
- desk edge
- chair silhouette
- action shelf
- near partition or plant

### Midground
- main focal workstation / table / desk cluster

### Background
- glass wall
- window band
- shelving
- secondary desks
- city glow

## 5.3 Circulation paths
The room must suggest how someone would walk through it.

Include:
- corridor lanes
- gaps between desk clusters
- path from entry to central floor
- path to meeting room or executive office

If no walking path is implied, the office will still read as arranged geometry.

## 5.4 Asymmetry
Avoid mirror-perfect layouts. Premium offices feel intentionally designed, not grid-generated.

Allowed asymmetry:
- one meeting room offset to one side
- one workstation bay denser than another
- lounge zone on only one perimeter edge
- delivery wall occupying one architectural side

# 6. HQ-Level Environment Direction

## 6.1 Replace district/tower read with office-floor read
The HQ overview should no longer suggest exterior towers first. It should suggest **an elevated view into a premium office floor**.

### Preferred visual interpretation
- angled cutaway office floor
- partially roofless or open-top presentation
- rooms defined by walls and furniture clusters
- windows at the perimeter, not skyline towers at the center

### Keep from the current build
- premium nocturnal lighting
- world-level hierarchy
- active-state glow logic
- project differentiation via accents

### Remove / reduce
- tower-first silhouette language
- abstract city-block masses without furniture read
- excessive exterior skyline dominance over interior office cues

## 6.2 HQ zone map
Recommended HQ macro layout:
- front/near edge: arrival + command strip
- center-left: active operations workstation floor
- center-right: project room cluster / private offices
- rear-left: glass meeting suite
- rear-right: archive / delivery / support wall
- perimeter: windows and mullions

## 6.3 Project representation at HQ level
Each project should read as a **room or office suite**, not a tower.

A project module should include:
- room boundary or glass enclosure
- 1 central desk/table cue
- 1–3 workstation or monitor cues
- project plaque on wall or entry edge
- color accent through lighting, not object type change

# 7. Project Room Direction

## 7.1 Room identity
A project room should feel like entering a real office suite with one clear purpose.

### Required elements
- one main desk or command table
- surrounding desks or support stations
- one delivery wall / artifact wall
- one window or glazed wall edge
- one architectural boundary explaining the room shape

## 7.2 Agent zones
Agents should live in recognizable workstation neighborhoods.

Examples:
- engineering bay: denser monitor clusters
- QA desk row: cleaner bench desks with evidence wall proximity
- reviewer corner: quieter side office or smaller executive station
- designer station: larger visual board / side display / pinup wall

## 7.3 Furniture-to-function mapping
Engineering should be able to map UI content to furniture.

| Function | Office object |
|---|---|
| active run | central command desk or meeting table display |
| agent card | workstation pod / desk station |
| artifact list | delivery wall slots / shelving plaques |
| ops info | side credenza display / wall console |
| messages | side monitor / comms console |
| actions | desk-edge controls / shelf capsules |

# 8. Lighting Direction

## 8.1 Overall lighting
Lighting should feel like after-hours office illumination.

Use:
- soft overhead architectural light
- monitor glow as local activity cue
- window-side city bounce
- under-desk or edge light only on hero objects

## 8.2 Lighting by room type
### Operations floor
- coolest light balance
- active monitor glow across multiple desks
- stronger central task illumination

### Meeting room
- more even overhead light
- slightly calmer than operations floor

### Executive office
- warmer wood reflections + cooler display light
- most premium contrast range

### Archive / delivery wall
- narrower wall washer lighting
- readable but secondary

## 8.3 State lighting rule
State color should decorate office objects, not replace them.

Correct:
- blocked = warning strip on a monitor bank or desk edge
- waiting = amber call light on communications surface
- active = cyan seam beneath monitor rail

Incorrect:
- entire room red
- giant neon floor outline everywhere

# 9. Immediate Visual Cues That Make It Read as an Office

These are mandatory because they do the most interpretive work for the least effort:
1. visible desks with chairs
2. monitor silhouettes with screen glow
3. glass meeting-room enclosure
4. perimeter windows with mullions
5. distinct corridor / walkway between furniture groups
6. wall-mounted shelving, whiteboard, or delivery wall
7. one large executive desk or conference table anchoring the space

If these seven cues are present, the environment will read as an office much faster than with abstract lighting tricks alone.

# 10. Implementation Priorities for Engineering

## Priority 1 — Architectural read
Add floor zoning, walls/partitions, windows, and corridor logic before adding more effects.

## Priority 2 — Furniture read
Replace abstract modules with desks, chairs, monitor clusters, meeting tables, credenzas, and shelving silhouettes.

## Priority 3 — Room-type clarity
Make HQ, project room, meeting room, and executive office visually distinct through furniture and boundary logic.

## Priority 4 — Premium finish
Apply polished material layering, localized light seams, glass reflections, and controlled night ambience.

## Priority 5 — State integration
Map live/blocked/waiting states into realistic office objects like displays, edge lights, signage, and console strips.

# 11. Acceptance Criteria

The office-world direction is correct only if:
1. A first-time viewer would describe the HQ as an office floor, not a city model or abstract board.
2. Workstation areas clearly contain desks, chairs, and monitor setups.
3. Rooms are defined by walls, glass partitions, windows, and circulation paths.
4. Project zones feel like office suites rather than detached glowing tiles.
5. Premium atmosphere comes from materials, architecture, and lighting, not from generic sci-fi effects.
6. Engineering can translate each object in the scene into a recognizable office asset or UI component.

## Failure signs
Revise the environment if:
- the room still reads primarily as blocks on a platform
- desks are not clearly visible as desks
- there are no chairs, windows, or walls
- there is no recognizable meeting room or executive-office language
- the world feels more like an exterior skyline than an interior workplace
